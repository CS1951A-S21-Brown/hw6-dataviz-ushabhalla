    Written Questions
  1. This dashboard addresses the questions presented in that it visualizes the number of football games per year, the winning percentage for the top 10 nations, and the top performing teams of the last two FIFA World Cups. In the first graph, I made a connected scatter graph where each point was a (year, number of games) pair. You can hover over each point to see the data numbers. In the second graph, I used a bar graph to visualize the winning percentage for the top 10 performing nations. For the third graph, I again used a bar graph where each bar can be toggled between the number of wins or the winning percentage of the top performing teams, where each team must have won at least 3 games. The fourth graph is a bubble map graph visualiztion of the teams on the third graph, where the data is toggled in accordance with the third graph. Note that for the last question, all teams with less than three games were omitted so that teams that won only one or two games would not skew the results.

    4. Accessible Data Visualization
  4. Given that the nature of this assignment is to learn how to use SVG graphs instead of image graphs, we have already started off on a strong foot with regards to accessibility, because the graph elements can be accessed by screen readers for those who are visually impaired or blind. However, there are definitely elements of the visualization that could be improved on to make this more accessible. To start off with, screen readers and other assistive technology cannot access overlays (such as those created by Tooltips), and therefore miss out on a lot of the information about individual data points as stated in the reading "A Comprehensive Guide to Accessible Data Visualization". The same can be said for highly interactive layouts, as it is difficult to select options and click on buttons while using those technologies. Furthermore, the descriptions and titles of the graphs are sufficient for those who know what the data is, but aren't extremely descriptive, and likely would be difficult to interpret on their own. Finally, while the colors chosen for the graphs were picked to maximize my own perception of the aesthetics of the site, they likely add no information to those who are color-blind, and could have been chosen with greater intentionality.
  5. One of the main stages in which I should have taken steps to make my dashboard more accessible is when I created the color palette for the graphs. I picked it based entirely off of my own preferred colors to view, when I should have taken into account the need for increased contrast for those who are visually impaired or color-blind. The main factor that kept me from changing the colors is that most fluid color scales seem to pose this problem, and extremely high contrast and discrete color scales are sometimes more difficult to interpret when looking at continuous data and are also less visually appealing (at least in my opinion).
